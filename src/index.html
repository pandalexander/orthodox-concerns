The Council of Jerusalem 1672, Decree II:

Wherefore, the witness also of the Catholic Church is, we believe, not of inferior authority to that of the Divine Scriptures. Not Orthodox? Not Saved

The Eastern Orthodox church formally holds that you are not saved if you are not Orthodox.

To illustrate this point, I will be drawing from The Synodikon of Orthodoxy, a text heavily associated with the decisions of the Seventh Ecumenical Council. It is to be annually read on the Sunday of Orthodoxy, the first Sunday of Lent.

From the titles of the Feast Day on which it is read, it is clearly being associated with the very identity of Eastern Orthodox Christianity, and of what it means to be an Eastern Orthodox Christian.

The Synodikon clearly addresses all who are not in the Eastern Church in the section from 1583, signed by the three chiefest Patriarchs, of Constantinople (and so Russia by inclusion for at the time it was a Metropolia under Constantinople), Alexandria, and Jerusalem, respectively:

That whoever does not confess with heart and mind that he is a child of the Eastern Church baptized in Orthodox style, and that the Holy Spirit proceeds out of only the Father, essentially and hypostatically, as Christ says in the Gospel, shall be outside our Church and shall be anathematized.

Concerning communion:

That whoever says that our Lord Jesus Christ at the Mystic Supper had unleavened bread (made without yeast), like that of the Jews, and not leavened bread, that is to say, bread raised with yeast, let him depart far from us and let him be anathema as one having Jewish views and those of Apolinarios and bringing dogmas of the Armenians into our Church, on which account let him be doubly anathema.

Concerning calendars:

That whoever does not follow the customs of the Church as the Seven Holy Ecumenical Councils decreed, and Holy Pascha, and the Menologion (an ecclesiastical calendar prescribing feastdays and fasting periods) with which they did well in making it a law that we should follow it, and wishes to follow the newly-invented Paschalion and the New Menologion (i.e. the Gregorian calendar) of the atheist astronomers of the Pope, and opposes all those things and wishes to overthrow and destroy the dogmas and customs of the Church which have been handed down by our fathers, let him suffer anathema and be put out of the Church of Christ and out of the congregation of the Faithful .

So, according to the Synodikon, you cannot be saved if you:

  • Aren’t part of the orthodox church
  • Believe Jesus ate leavened bread at The Last Supper
  • Disagree about the calendar

Lastly, The Seventh Ecumenical Council speaks of those who refuse to bow down and kiss icons with affection. as God forsaken and God-hated heretics. Session VI, The Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers, 2nd Series, Vol. 14,p. 572, 573, and 542. This is clearly a serious condemnation of every professing Christian that refuses to bow down and kiss icons.

Does Anathema Really Mean Hell?

Some may be inclined to say that an anathema is less severe than being sent straight to hell. Let me explain how the Orthodox church defines anathema.

The Seventh Ecumenical Council:

“Now anathema is nothing less than complete separation from God”

Second Nicaea, "The Letter from the Synod to the Emperor and Empress", from The Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers, 2nd Series, Vol. 14, p. 573

An Orthodox Canon Law Reference Book describes an anathema as the most severe ecclesiastical sanction where the guilty person is cut off from the Church and from salvation (compiled by Fr. Vasile Mihai, Holy Cross Orthodox Press, 2014; pg 49)

He continues by stating: Anathema is parallel to the death penalty in secular society, as spiritual death is seen by the Church as analogous to physical death.

Notice, these anathemas are not calls to repentance. They are a judicial sentencing followed by their corresponding judgments.

As Canon XIII (in this instance dealing with those who misuse monastic properties) from the Seventh Ecumenical Council states, to be “excommunicated” is to be:

condemned from the Father, and the Son, and the Holy Ghost, and assigned their place where the worm dieth not and the fire is not quenched...

The Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers, 2nd Series, Vol. 14, p. 564.

Not Christ's Righteousness That Justifies

The righteousness you possess and maintain by virtue of your works justifies you before God

The Council of Jerusalem 1672, Decree IX:

We believe no one to be saved without faith. And by faith we mean the right notion that is in us concerning God and divine things, which, working by love, that is to say, by [observing] the Divine commandments, justifieth us with Christ; and without this [faith] it is impossible to please God.

Essentially, saving faith is reduced to having and affirming the right information (notion), not a reliant trust in and on Christ. It's more a belief about rather than a belief in.

After defining faith as notional, it adds works. So, salvation = right information + works.

The Council of Jerusalem 1672, Decree XIII

We believe a man to be not simply justified through faith alone, but through faith which worketh through love, that is to say, through faith and works. But [the notion] that faith fulfilling the function of a hand layeth hold on the righteousness which is in Christ, and applieth it unto us for salvation, we know to be far from all Orthodoxy. For faith so understood would be possible in all, and so none could miss salvation, which is obviously false. But on the contrary, we rather believe that it is not the correlative of faith , but the faith which is in us, justifieth through works, with Christ. But we regard works not as witnesses certifying our calling, but as being fruits in themselves, through which faith becometh efficacious, and as in themselves meriting, through the Divine promises {cf. 2 Corinthians 5:10} that each of the Faithful may receive what is done through his own body , whether it be good or bad...

What is the correlative of faith spoken of here? It is Jesus Christ and his righteousness.

This understanding is confirmed when we note that Decithius is responding to Cyril Lucaris’ claim that the correlative of faith is the righteousness of Christ. As the confession attributed to Lucaris states: “but when we say 'by faith' we understand the correlative or object of Faith which is the righteousness of Christ”. According to Lucaris, then, it is the righteousness of Christ which justifies - a righteousness which is received by faith as faith’s correlative .

The Eastern Orthodox Church expressly denies that Christ's righteousness justifies a person. The righteousness of Christ, and even Christ himself, is not sufficient to justify according to Eastern Orthodox canonical theology, but are insufficient and must be added to. In their doctrine, faith justifies through works with Christ.

In other words, works are meritorious. The faith they speak of is more like a personal virtue, since it's not the correlative of faith that justifies. Instead, it's the faith a person possesses by the exercise of their own natural ability to have faith, like when a person is loyal, that enables works to become meirtorious. So, it is a kind of loyalty to the information about Jesus plus one's works that justifies a person with Christ.

Epiphanius Bishop of Salamis (born AD 310) writing to John of Jerusalem.

Moreover, I have heard that certain persons have this grievance against me: When I accompanied you to the holy place called Bethel, there to join you in celebrating the Collect, after the use of the Church, I came to a villa called Anablatha and, as I was passing, saw a lamp burning there. Seeing this, and being loth that an image of a man should be hung up in Christ’s church contrary to the teaching of the Scriptures, I tore it asunder and advised the custodians of the place to use it as a winding sheet for some poor person. I have now sent the best that I could find, and I beg that you will order the presbyter of the place to take the curtain which I have sent from the hands of the Reader, and that you will afterwards give directions that curtains of the other sort—opposed as they are to our religion—shall not be hung up in any church of Christ. A man of your uprightness should be careful to remove an occasion of offence unworthy alike of the Church of Christ and of those Christians who are committed to your charge.
